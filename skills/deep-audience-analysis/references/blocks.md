# The Eight Blocks

Write each block in full. Under each item below, produce concrete, situation-
level content — not adjectives. Where you assert a specific the user has not
confirmed, tag it `[HYPOTHESIS]`. Preserve the customer's own language in the
audience's language; do not sanitize it.

Depth check: if a block reads like generic advice that could apply to any niche,
it is too shallow. Redo it with named situations, inner monologue, and specifics.

---

## Block 1 — Identity, values & worldview

Who the core customer *is*, beneath demographics.

- Self-image: who they believe they are, who they want to be seen as.
- Core beliefs — including the near-religious one they organize life around
  (e.g. "if I do X and endure, I'll be safe"). State their "deal with the world."
- Relationship to money and spending.
- Lifestyle and daily reality.
- Dreams, wins, failures; who they are *outside* the context of your product.

End with **Block insight:** one sentence that reframes how to see this customer.

## Block 2 — Pains & fears

- The concrete recurring situations where the pain bites (name them; describe
  the scene).
- How the pain shows up emotionally *and* physically/somatically — this sensory
  material is what makes content feel "seen."
- What they are afraid of, and what they are trying to avoid at all costs.

## Block 3 — Barriers, myths & past experience

- What keeps them stuck / stops them starting.
- The points where they typically give up ("points of failure").
- Myths and mistaken beliefs they hold about the problem or the solution.
- What they have already tried and how it let them down (often a negative prior
  experience with a competitor or DIY attempt).
- What they categorically refuse to let happen.

## Block 4 — Desired transformation

- The before-state and the after-state they are actually buying.
- What "solved" looks and feels like in their own terms.

## Block 5 — Triggers & decision points

The highest-value block.

- The exact life circumstances/events that convert a background problem into an
  active search — the moment "thinking about the pain" becomes "doing something."
- Rational buy criteria (what, on paper, makes them choose).
- Emotional buy criteria (what makes them buy even without a rational push).

These points power content, ads, and live sales conversations — you speak to the
moment, not the abstract problem.

## Block 6 — Hidden psychology / resistance

- What *actually* stops the purchase, beneath the stated objection.
- Secondary gains: why staying unsolved is quietly beneficial to them (identity,
  comfort, avoiding blame, keeping an excuse).
- The internal argument they have with themselves at the point of buying.

## Block 7 — Customer language

Raw material for copy and content. Keep everything verbatim, in the audience's
language.

- Exact words and phrases they use for the problem, the desire, and the solution.
- Slang, jargon, and filler that signal "one of us."
- Words/phrases that build trust vs. words that repel or sound salesy.

## Block 8 — Golden segments

Your audience is not homogeneous. A minority of segments produce the majority of
easy, high-value sales.

- Identify each meaningful segment.
- For each, write a positioning-ready description (specific enough to drop
  straight into a landing page or offer).
- Close with the scoring table:

| Segment | Pain acuity (1-5) | Willingness/ability to pay (1-5) | Actively searching (1-5) | Priority score |

Rank by `Priority score`, and explicitly name the top segment as the primary
focus for offer, content, and spend.
